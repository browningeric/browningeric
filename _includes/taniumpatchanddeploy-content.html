Patch and Deploy are two of the most widely used (>30m endpoints managed) IT Operations products at Tanium. I was tasked with ownership of their user experience. This led to a number of significant features, improvements, and activities over several years.

Why weren't users using automation features?

I co-lead a research effort with engineering and product leadership to answer the question of why some of our automation features were not seeing much usage. Contextual interview sessions were held to understand the mental models and workflows of new and veteran users. We observed routine deployment tasks, and sought input on workflows.

Our cross functional team then workshopped to analyze the data and understand the stories that were emerging, using affinity diagrams and mapping user journeys.

We learned that automation is scary to IT Ops professionals. The complexities of their environments and the stakes involved meant that they were unlikely to adopt much automation, no matter how well we built it.

First Solution: A Readiness Guide

I prototyped a design for a recurring display of readiness to perform OS Patching. I had learned that a true "easy button" scared users, but helping them understand exactly what was going to happen and why with their regular patch cadence would be a huge benefit.

OS Patches are released on a regular cadence. Operators need to know which machines are ready to be updated, which do not meet some prequalifications, and tooling issues or anything else that may lead to unexpected results.

My early versions were well received, but implied too much of a linear flow whereas the actual workflow was more of a broad checklist.

Revising based on Feedback

The final iteration focused on the broad categories the most likely to cause problems or unexpected results for operators in a patch deployment scenario.

This data already existed in the platform, but required users to have the knowledge to know where to look and the planning to seek out everything on time. Offering all the relevant data in a simple obvious display was great step forward. Each category can be expanded, to view more details about potential issues and begin remediation.

Second Solution: End User Self Service

The second solution was a desktop application where users themselves would be able to choose what software to install or update and when on their machines.

Giving end users control in some scenarios greatly reduced chance of environment-wide mistakes and freed up operators to tackle bigger challenges.

Results

These features helped improve satisfaction and usage of the modules, as measured by SUS scores, direct customer feedback, and customer service-team heuristics.

I worked on a number of different additional features including a front-end redesign, localization efforts, and support for new OS platforms.
